Job Aid

California Provisions on Preventing Discrimination and Harassment

Purpose: Use this job aid to help you support a harassment-free workplace.

Use this information as a guide to California's Discrimination and Harassment laws.

Protected Characteristics

The California Fair Employment and Housing Act (FEHA) prohibits discrimination, harassment, and retaliation against applicants and employees, based on certain characteristics. In addition, it prohibits discrimination, harassment, and retaliation based on a perception that the applicant or employee has any of these characteristics or that the applicant or employee is associated with a person who has, or is perceived to have, any of these characteristics. It also prohibits harassment against volunteers and persons providing services pursuant to a contract. The protected characteristics include:

  • race
  • religious creed (see note 1)
  • color
  • national origin (see note 2)
  • ancestry
  • age (40 and older)
  • disability (mental and physical, including HIV and AIDS) (see note 3)
  • medical condition (see note 4)
  • genetic information (see note 5)
  • marital status (see note 6)
  • sex (including pregnancy, childbirth, breastfeeding or related medical conditions)
  • sexual orientation (heterosexuality, homosexuality, bisexuality)
  • gender
  • gender identity
  • gender expression
  • transgender status
  • military or veteran status (see note 7)
  • political activity or affiliation
  • requesting or taking a statutorily protected leave of absence

Workplace Harassment

Harassment includes, but is not limited to, the following, if such actions are based on one of the protected characteristics above:

  • verbal harassment, such as epithets, jokes, derogatory comments, or slurs
  • physical harassment, such as assault, impeding or blocking movement, or any physical interference with normal work or movement
  • visual harassment, such as derogatory posters, cartoons, or drawings
  • sexual favors, the exchange of which is a condition of an employment benefit
  • unwanted sexual advances

Sexual Harassment

The FEHA defines sexual harassment as harassment based on sex or of a sexual nature; gender harassment; and harassment based on pregnancy, childbirth, or related medical conditions. The definition of sexual harassment includes many forms of offensive behavior, including harassment of a person of the same gender as the harasser.

The following is a partial list of types of sexual harassment:

  • unwanted sexual advances
  • offering employment benefits in exchange for sexual favors
  • actual or threatened retaliation
  • leering; making sexual gestures; or displaying sexually suggestive objects, pictures, cartoons, or posters
  • making or using derogatory comments, epithets, slurs, or jokes related to someone's gender or gender expression
  • sexual comments including graphic comments about an individual's body
  • sexually degrading words used to describe an individual
  • suggestive or obscene letters, notes, messages, or invitations (either in paper or electronic form), and
  • physical touching or assault, as well as impeding or blocking movements

Sexual desire is not necessary.

Abusive Conduct

Abusive conduct is defined as the behavior of an employee or manager that is malicious and that is "hostile, offensive, and unrelated to an employer's legitimate business interests."

Examples include:

  • repeated verbal abuse
  • derogatory remarks
  • insults or epithets
  • physical conduct that is threatening, intimidating, or humiliating
  • undermining or sabotaging another person's work

Generally, a single action is not considered abusive conduct unless it is "especially severe and egregious."

Abusive conduct, if allowed to occur in the workplace, can result in harmful effects for victims and other employees, including:

  • increased stress
  • depression and anxiety
  • eating disorders
  • sleep disorders
  • physical ailments
  • other negative impacts to mental and physical health

Employees suffering from the harmful effects above can also experience decreased morale, lower productivity, disengagement, and high turnover.

Endnotes

1. Religious creed includes any traditionally recognized religion, as well as beliefs, observances, or practices, which an individual sincerely holds and which occupy in the individual's life a place of importance parallel to that of traditionally recognized religions. It encompasses all aspects of religious belief, observance, and practice, including religious dress and grooming practices.

2. California regulations define �national origin� to include an individual�s actual or perceived: (i) physical, cultural, or linguistic characteristics associated with a national origin group; (ii)  marriage to or association with persons of a national origin group; (iii) tribal affiliation; (iv) membership in or association with an organization identified with or seeking to promote the interests of a national origin group; (v) attendance or participation in schools, churches, temples, mosques, or other religious institutions generally used by persons of national origin group; and (vi) name that is associated with a national origin group.  Some examples of association-based and perception-based harassment and discrimination based on national origin include:  (i) harassment of an employee whose spouse is from Yemen; (ii) harassment of a Hispanic person by a colleague who mistakenly believes that the Hispanic person is Pakistani, or harassment of a Sikh man wearing a turban because the harasser mistakenly perceives him to be Muslim; and (iii) referring to a worker of Middle-Eastern descent as �Taliban� or �Arab.�

3. Mental and physical disabilities do not include sexual behavior disorders, compulsive gambling, kleptomania, pyromania, or psychoactive substance use disorders resulting from the current unlawful use of controlled substances or other drugs, unless the aforementioned would be considered disabilities under the federal Americans with Disabilities Act, as amended.

4. Medical condition means: (i) any health impairment related to or associated with a diagnosis of cancer or a record or history of cancer; or (ii) any scientifically or medically identifiable gene or chromosome, or combination or alteration thereof, that is known to be a cause of a disease or disorder in a person or the person's offspring, or that is determined to be associated with a statistically increased risk of development of a disease or disorder, and that is presently not associated with any symptoms of any disease or disorder; or (iii) inherited characteristics that may derive from the individual or family member, that are known to be a cause of a disease or disorder in a person or the person's offspring, or that are determined to be associated with a statistically increased risk of development of a disease or disorder, and that are presently not associated with any symptoms of any disease or disorder.

5. Genetic information means: an individual's genetic tests; the genetic tests of family members of the individual; the manifestation of a disease or disorder in family members of the individual; and/or the request for or receipt of genetic services or participation in clinical research that includes genetic services by an individual or any family member of an individual. Genetic information does not include information about the sex or age of any individual.

6. Marital status includes the state of marriage, non-marriage, divorce or dissolution, separation, widowhood, annulment, or other marital state, including the employment or lack of employment of the individual's spouse.

7. Military or veteran status means a member or veteran of the United States Armed Forces, United States Armed Forces Reserve, the United States National Guard, or the California National Guard.
